<h3>Hello and Welcome!</h3><p>I’ve had a lot of new readers subscribe because of my last post about Western States, so I thought it a good time to level set with what this Substack is about.</p><p>First off, you probably noticed this, but I don’t post often or on a normal cadence. I work as a full-time photographer and filmmaker in the running industry, and I don’t always have time to write. Because of this, it’s free to be here. I enjoy writing when I have the time, and it’s nice to have an audience. So enjoy the posts when they do happen, and interact with me if you like them!</p><p>Second, what do I talk about? It’s mostly me working through the problems I encounter in my career/life, which are often more mental than physical. If you’ve read back through my past work, you’ll get the idea. Sometimes it will be a thought exercise that I’m working through, sometimes it will simply be complaining, sometimes it will be sharing what inspires me, sometimes it will be about creativity, and other times it will be more technical. It won’t be focused on one area as it will be a reflection of myself, which I’d like to think is multifaceted.</p><p>A couple of guarantees, though. It will never be written by AI. That would defeat the purpose of my enjoying the writing. I won’t try to sell you on anything. I don’t plan to monetize this, so my only incentive here is to entertain you and me, and if I’m being honest, I mostly am doing it just to entertain myself. And if you ever want to ask me about something, I’ll respond.</p><p>With that out of the way, let’s get into today’s topic.</p><figure><img src="https://ik.imagekit.io/tony/blog/stronger-together/c1d38d87-4329-473c-9a12-8aff70939b02_4096x2731.jpeg?tr=w-1456" alt="Stronger Together" loading="lazy"></figure><h1>NonDē and Running Media</h1><p>I recently stumbled upon the Substack world of NonDē, led mainly by a guy named <a href="https://tedhope.substack.com/">Ted Hope and his Substack, Hope for Film</a>. He claims that Indie or Independent filmmaking  never lived up to its promise and is dead. Instead, he hopes to facilitate the next version of this. NonDē or Not Dependent means to have a thriving film ecosystem that isn’t dependent on corporations or the Hollywood systems of the past. Don’t worry too much about this NonDē concept, as I’m still trying to wrap my head around his message and this movement. It was a post from a different author about NonDē culture that got me thinking. The <a href="https://substack.com/@tedgioia/p-168102914">post was from Ted Gioia</a> where he shared this graphic as a summary.</p><figure><img src="https://ik.imagekit.io/tony/blog/stronger-together/bf69412d-8f4a-459d-94bd-448e15c3fee4_1110x724.png?tr=w-1456" alt="Stronger Together" loading="lazy"></figure><h2>Artist vs Entrepreneur</h2><p>This NonDē movement and the thoughts on Indie culture feel more tied into being an artist, but that’s not fully me. I like working with Brands and teams of creative people to make something bigger come to life. So, how does all this fit into a career path more like that? This and all aspects of the Indie culture above have been bouncing around in my brain recently. </p><h3>End Isolation</h3><p>Freelancing can be quite isolating at times. I miss the collaboration of a team, and when I do get to work with others, it’s a joy. I’ve been thinking a lot recently about how to bring the community of trail creatives together, and I hope to start bringing these ideas to life soon. There is a lot of power in bringing us together. So much we can share, so much we can learn from each other, and so much we can support each other with. </p><p>Sharing can be scary, though. The past has instilled a Scarcity mindset in us, and it can be hard to kick it. If I share my “secrets,” how will I keep my advantage? Should I sell this knowledge instead of sharing it freely? How do I win? These questions are the lingering scar of scarcity, and their answers are meaningless in abundance. Abundance is a practice, and some days we’ll get it right, others we’ll get it wrong. As long as we can trend in the right direction. </p><figure><img src="https://ik.imagekit.io/tony/blog/stronger-together/39eca3e5-de5a-4e1c-90d6-78f5f5ff9271_4096x2731.jpeg?tr=w-1456" alt="Stronger Together" loading="lazy"></figure><h3>Build Platforms and Alliances</h3><p>I remember not long ago, a group of creators started a joint Patreon to use existing platforms to create a different kind of collective work model. I wonder how they’re doing. Either way, this is an area that we’re lacking. Patreon and the like are focused on single users or brands. It’s not their fault, though. So much focus is on the individual. That’s the direction we’ve been going in as a society. Leaning more and more into individualism. </p><p>Influencers, creators, solopreneurs, freelancers, whatever you want to call them, it’s on the rise. We’re leaving the old company model behind in search of time and location flexibility, being our own bosses, and with the hope that we can make it big. This brings us back to ending isolation. Time is a flat circle. Next thing you know, we’ll form collectives of individuals working toward a shared goal, and we’ll call it “a company!” Genius! Joking aside, what will this new middle ground be? Can we have collectives and alliances of individuals that can truly be themselves as well as share in the collective growth? What kinds of platforms might we need to accomplish this? What’s next?</p><figure><img src="https://ik.imagekit.io/tony/blog/stronger-together/252bcb14-e3dc-4215-9730-f0afd13ccb9c_4096x2731.jpeg?tr=w-1456" alt="Stronger Together" loading="lazy"></figure><h3>Defend Our Rights</h3><p>I believe our creations are ours. We should own what we create. As a photographer, it’s simple. We own the photos we take, and we can license them to companies for use. I’m sure there is nuance in there, and not every photoshoot needs to be this way, but I believe this should be the default. We shouldn’t be scared to push back on clients who want copyright. If a client wants copyright, they know these images have more value, and I don’t think we should give up that value so easily.</p><p>This concept gets more difficult to understand with larger productions. Who owns something when multiple contributors caused its creation? We haven’t yet created good structures for this type of work. Usually, it flows up to the company that paid for the production. But is there a better way? A way that supports the people who created it? Maybe a fractional ownership model. More thought needs to be put into this.</p><figure><img src="https://ik.imagekit.io/tony/blog/stronger-together/ace65217-77d6-4d8e-addc-cc970c30784d_4096x2731.jpeg?tr=w-1456" alt="Stronger Together" loading="lazy"></figure><h3>Create an Ecosystem of Risk Taking</h3><p>How do we encourage risk-taking, fresh perspectives, and innovative work? Support. The more support you have, the more willing you are to take a risk. A recent example I can think of was at the Miwok 100k. I hired a second photographer to help photograph the race. We had to capture every runner as well as more of the “marketing shots.” Having the support of two photographers meant that we could dedicate some of that day to taking risks and trying something new. I shot a lot of the race with a fish-eye lens. I knew every runner was still going to get a typical trail running shot, and maybe they would also get something a little different. Not a risk I would have taken if it were just me out there.</p><p>With support, we can grow ourselves and the industry. This brings us back to the previous topics of bringing us together in community and building platforms and alliances to support each other.</p><figure><img src="https://ik.imagekit.io/tony/blog/stronger-together/b09198db-fa44-4469-89d5-3e19c56bb327_2731x4096.jpeg?tr=w-1456" alt="Stronger Together" loading="lazy"></figure><h3>Support Initiatives</h3><p>I’m always looking out for people doing things differently. It’s exciting to find those smart people out there, and one person who keeps landing in my feed is Yancey, one of the founders of Kickstarter. He recently created a new Kickstarter-like platform for artists and collectives to sell their work. Check out <a href="https://www.metalabel.com/">MetaLabel</a> if you have a second, but more interestingly, he’s been pushing for a new kind of legal structure. <a href="https://artistcorps.substack.com/about">A-Corps, or Artist Corporations</a>, would help artists and collectives finally have a corporate structure that reflects the way they want to work. The concept of A-Corp is still in its infancy, but the idea is exciting.</p><p>And this now ties us back to NonDē. An initiative I’m infinitely curious about. Mostly because I wonder if it’s possible. Can we really change? If I’ve learned anything, it’s that we can’t do it alone. If change is coming, it’s coming because we’re all moving together.</p><p>I’m not sure where I’ll land with all of this, but <strong>if this post sparked something in you, reach out. If you’ve been grappling with these questions and ideas too, reach out. If you just want to connect with another creative, reach out. Let’s build a better future for all of us, together.</strong></p>
