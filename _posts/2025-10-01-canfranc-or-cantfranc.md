<p>Last weekend, in a small town nestled within epic mountains, the world championships of trail and mountain running took place. Canfranc isn’t a place I'd ever heard of before this event. It was the center of the races for the weekend, but the town itself was too small to hold everyone, so most people stayed 20 minutes away, just outside the valley in a bigger town called Jaca, pronounced “Haca.” My wife, Sammy, came with me this time and was my most gracious driver during the races. We had a lovely time in Spain, and there are a few things that stood out to me that I’d like to lay out for you.</p><h3>The Pyrenees</h3><p>…are beautiful! This mountain range wasn’t really on my radar, but now I really want to go back and explore. It doesn’t have the epic glaciers that Chamonix has, but it does have epic mountains, vast expanses, dramatic reliefs, and rock coloring and formations that feel like the American Southwest had a baby with the Alps. Like the Alps, it has huts and great walks. The Camino de Santiago walked through many of the places where we were hanging out. I’m definitely going to scheme a little hut-to-hut trip here someday.</p><figure><img src="https://ik.imagekit.io/tony/blog/canfranc-or-cantfranc/1200f0ba-9125-4613-9569-19ffce1ca4fa_4096x1039.jpeg?tr=w-1456" alt="Canfranc or Can'tfranc?" loading="lazy"></figure><h3>The Competition</h3><p>…was deep! The races were unlike any race I had been to before. You can’t just sign up for a World’s race. Everyone out there was an elite athlete in their country. The races were so fast, and if you weren’t having your best day ever, you easily fell 10s/20s of spots. Seeing some of the best athletes we have in the states getting in the 30s and 40s was kinda crazy. It led to some great racing and close finishes, although we still had a few blowouts, *cough* Katie *cough*.</p><figure><img src="https://ik.imagekit.io/tony/blog/canfranc-or-cantfranc/87b72a8d-1175-4646-9329-481db64fb7aa_2731x4096.jpeg?tr=w-1456" alt="Canfranc or Can'tfranc?" loading="lazy"></figure><h3>The Team Aspect</h3><p>…was super fun and added a new dynamic to the whole weekend. If you were the third person in your country in a race, you still had to work hard even if you were in 20th or 40th place. Every minute counted for a country’s top 3 runners, and it added a bit to the excitement. Cross country fans won’t be surprised by this, but for a trail running, this was a bit different. It was also fun to see big groups of athletes walking around together in town, all dressed in the same colors. You could spot someone on the German team or the Irish team from a mile away.</p><figure><img src="https://ik.imagekit.io/tony/blog/canfranc-or-cantfranc/689c8b25-5c3c-40ec-8062-57305c493e32_2731x4096.jpeg?tr=w-1456" alt="Canfranc or Can'tfranc?" loading="lazy"></figure><h5>however</h5><p>Not every team was equal. I’m not sure how accommodations are planned, but some teams definitely seemed to get the short end of the stick with hostel-like places, while some teams, like Team USA, were in beautiful hotels. Many trail teams are an afterthought when compared to their track and field counterparts, so I heard some complaints about the lack of support, terrible race kit options, horrendous food, and many other things that definitely didn’t set up the athletes for success. Feels like an unfair advantage for a team to just have a ton more money than another team, but I guess this isn’t that different from normal trail running.</p><h3>Speaking of the Food</h3><p>It was a shock. As a plant-based eater, I struggled quite a bit. Spain, or at least the area we were in, was meat-heavy. Finding a vegetable was challenging, and Jaca, being a small town, meant places that did have vegetarian fare sold out quickly. There was also the midday siesta that made eating times very specific and not conducive to whenever you get off the mountains. All this, paired with the off-season ski town of Jaca having many places closed for the fall, led to a shitty diet for me. If I had to do it again, I’d stock up on groceries and pre-plan meals.</p><h3>A Vision Toward the Olympics</h3><p>I was told that if any part of trail running has a shot of getting into the Olympics, it’s this format. The Olympics want standardization and rules. Which feels a little antithetical to Trail Culture. I get it, though. I would love to see some form of trail running in the Olympics as well. It won’t be the trail running I know and love, but it will still bring a lot to our sport. That said, Worlds is trying to apply and enforce rules to get there. Some of these rules are impossible to enforce, though, and so I want to look at a few examples I saw.</p><figure><img src="https://ik.imagekit.io/tony/blog/canfranc-or-cantfranc/7103fe94-5ab0-41e7-99d1-26e9f11b6710_4096x2731.jpeg?tr=w-1456" alt="Canfranc or Can'tfranc?" loading="lazy"></figure><h4>Media Regulations</h4><p>Like the Olympics, Brands can’t activate with their athletes, although it’s definitely not as strict as the Olympics, but it means many athletes can’t be celebrated by their sponsors. This race doesn’t pay out, and with media restrictions like this, sometimes athletes can’t make money on sponsor bonuses from doing well. Many athletes don’t prioritize Worlds because of their sponsors and contracts. Does it have to be this way? Could we start doing better in trail running instead of following track and field’s path?</p><p>Another silly rule was the restricted areas for the media. We got a PDF explaining how some areas were off limits due to athlete safety. Seems reasonable, and I avoided those areas like a good little rule follower. Except I come to find out many media people went to those places and got some cool shots because no one else was there. And of course, there was no punishment and no one cared. That will be the last time I follow the rules.</p><p>My final media restriction gripe was the finish line vests. They only allowed 20 media people into the finish line, and so you had to request ahead of time to get a vest that allowed you in. Not a bad plan except that once someone left the finish line and returned the vest, no one else could get that vest. So after the top people finished, the media corral slowly dwindled to nobody. No one was there to watch women finish beyond 10th place. And with more than 20 countries participating, not every country could even have a photographer in the corral. It felt super restrictive for no reason. I have a lot more I didn’t like about the finish line, but I’ll leave it at that.</p><figure><img src="https://ik.imagekit.io/tony/blog/canfranc-or-cantfranc/98389084-e4ba-4174-bb82-d67347589bf6_4096x2731.jpeg?tr=w-1456" alt="Canfranc or Can'tfranc?" loading="lazy"></figure><h4>Athlete Kits</h4><p>Athletes had to wear kits from their team sponsor, Nike in the case of Team USA. But it was easy for the athletes to wear a running vest that covered up the Nike logo and had their Brand’s logo instead, so it kinda of seemed like a silly rule to me. I also hate this restriction because why do we have to force athletes to wear gear they are unfamiliar with and maybe don’t like? At least they were allowed to wear whatever shoes they wanted. It feels like we’re not really interested in athletes performing their best or prioritising their experience; instead, they are our little billboard advertisements running around a mountain.</p><figure><img src="https://ik.imagekit.io/tony/blog/canfranc-or-cantfranc/59e2241e-a396-4bbd-a9fe-370532df7d09_4096x2731.jpeg?tr=w-1456" alt="Canfranc or Can'tfranc?" loading="lazy"></figure><h4>No Aid Outside Aid Stations</h4><p>What constitutes aid outside of an aid station? It seems like it should be obvious, but also so hard to define. You can’t give or take anything to or from a runner, that’s pretty clear-cut. But deciding if someone is pacing an athlete is harder. We’ve decided on a time or distance rule of not running with an athlete for 100m or whatever it is, but this seems silly. I saw a woman somewhere between 10th and 20th get yellow carded, a time penalty, because a friend or even just a random fan ran with them 100 meters into an aid station. They weren’t even trying to help the athlete; they were just cheering and waving their flag. This kind of upset me. Who cares? Did that little jog really help that athlete beyond what anyone else was getting? There were big crowds all over the course cheering for people. The French were out in force, cheering harder every time a French athlete came through. Is that unfair? What about the huge crowds dissipating after the top runners came through? Is that unfair for the rest of the field? What about the remote areas of the course where someone could (and did) run with their athlete for some miles? That seems unfair, but no one was there to enforce it. Having some guy pull a yellow card on the 15th female for having a fan excitedly run with her for 100m felt like overkill and a power trip. If we can’t enforce this rule everywhere, maybe we can be more relaxed about it in places where it doesn’t matter.</p><figure><img src="https://ik.imagekit.io/tony/blog/canfranc-or-cantfranc/bc05a088-701c-40a0-8f69-28a4d9b0315c_2731x4096.jpeg?tr=w-1456" alt="Canfranc or Can'tfranc?" loading="lazy"></figure><h3>I’ll still go back</h3><p>Despite all my complaints, I had a great time and I’ll be back. I’ll definitely be back to the Pyrenees as I want to explore those mountains. And I hope I can go back to Worlds in 2027 in Cape Town! Stu, the race director for UTCT and a friend, is helping organize it, and I hope I can chat with him about some of these topics, but I also know he’s an excellent race director and maybe is already thinking of things he wants to do differently. Can’t wait to check back in 2 years from now!</p>
