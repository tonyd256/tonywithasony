<p>I’ve been having the conversation about Day Rates a lot recently. There is very little information out there on what you should be charging as a freelance photographer, videographer, or really any freelancer. I think it’s probably hidden because of the fear of being undercut or maybe it’s just one of those things we don’t talk about because we’re carrying the baggage of money talks being taboo from earlier generations. Either way, I want to dive into how I think about Day Rates and how I calculate mine. I’m going to dive right into it but after, I’ll talk a bit about why I’m publishing this along with a bunch of considerations and context.</p><figure><img src="https://ik.imagekit.io/tony/blog/a-deep-dive-into-day-rates/2c61b7d5-7fe6-4c3e-ba33-b48577f80f1c_4096x2731.jpeg?tr=w-1456" alt="Enjoy these random photos from a recent SFRC run while you read." loading="lazy"><figcaption>Enjoy these random photos from a recent SFRC run while you read.</figcaption></figure><h1>The Day Rate</h1><p>If you search the internet for how much you should be charging, you’ll either come across a lot of numbers that have no meaning and seem pretty low or a vague statement that’s like, “Charge by what you need to sustain your lifestyle divided by how many days you want to work.” This statement always bothered me. First, “sustain my lifestyle” really doesn’t leave much room to grow, save, invest, start a family, etc. Second, “how many days I want to work” is equally vague. I want to work quite a bit of days but I don’t always (almost never) get to choose those days. So let’s actually look at this statement and make some calculations.</p><h3>How many days can you actually work?</h3><p>We have 365 days a year but most people only work 5 days a week, plus 15 days vacation and we’ll throw in 10 sick days. So really, we have about 235 days of workable time. BUT you have to remember we’re not working all of these days despite what may be our best efforts to do so. As a freelancer, we’re also CEOs, CMOs, COOs, CFOs, sales people, customer success people, check chasers, idea pitchers, and sometimes on top of all this, we have second jobs! So, realistically, we get a quarter of that time to actually work for clients as the rest of the time we’re working on the biz or trying to get clients. 58.75 days. We’ll round to 60.</p><figure><img src="https://ik.imagekit.io/tony/blog/a-deep-dive-into-day-rates/31c3c686-aa4f-458a-880e-a304f2e484d7_4096x2731.jpeg?tr=w-1456" alt="A Deep Dive into Day Rates" loading="lazy"></figure><h3>What do you need to live?</h3><p>Now how much do we need to live? Check your rent and your past few credit card payments for a good baseline. My rent is $1450, not bad for SF. My personal expenses are about $2k a month including grocery shopping, utilities, going out every now and then, gas, tolls, and whatever the heck else I want! Stop snooping on my life! OK, OK. Let’s say $3500 a month to sustain my lifestyle. Yours maybe very different. I live in an expensive city and I think I’m still pretty low. When I first started this career, I lived in a community house where I shared a room with another person. We can do wild things to start living our passion but I don’t think it’s sustainable. Eventually, most of us want partners, houses, and retirement savings.</p><p>On that note, let’s add a meager 10% for our savings so we can first build a runway buffer as we won’t always have work and then be able to add to a retirement fund and invest. And then of course we have the camera gear and accessories we need to actually do the jobs. Let’s add another 10% for continuing gear upgrades and maintenance. Ah, but then the government sees you living a pretty good life and they want their cut! 20% more! A very rounded tax estimate. Taxes are so friggin complicated when you get in the weeds, so I’m just making it simple for now. We’re now close to $5000 a month, $60,000 a year! That’s some poverty levels for San Francisco. I think for a new Freelancer, this is a good benchmark to aim for. Strive to get a 50-60k year under your belt as quickly as you can. And of course, this divided by our days we can work, we get about a $1000 day rate. Not bad. Again, I think new freelancers should try to get up to a 1k day rate as quick as they can.</p><figure><img src="https://ik.imagekit.io/tony/blog/a-deep-dive-into-day-rates/4e5dbe4e-f790-4132-a5e7-224ead939dd6_2731x4096.jpeg?tr=w-1456" alt="A Deep Dive into Day Rates" loading="lazy"></figure><h3>Beyond the Day Rate</h3><p>It’s not just about a day rate though. All gigs need more time than a “day.” Days you’re traveling aren’t days you can be working. Days where you have client meetings, virtually or otherwise, aren’t days that you can be out for another client all day. So we should be charging for these things as they eat into our workable days. Travel Day Fees, which is different from Travel Expenses, and Pre-production Fees are all widely accepted things to charge for.</p><p>I laugh sometimes when clients just ask for my rate sheet or just my day rate. I try to convince them it won’t help and that understanding and proposing a budget based on the project and deliverables is much more accurate. I digress.</p><h3>A Day isn’t just a day</h3><p>Now let’s get into the fun part. I work a lot of events in the trail running world. 100 kilometer and 100 mile races where runners are out there 8-24+ hours! That, plus all the time you need to capture them before and after these races. We’re not talking about the standard 7-8 hour day. These longer days deserve higher rates. And when you’re outdoors, running around capturing runners, and sometimes not sleeping much or at all for days in a row, there is a recovery time. I can’t do another big gig for at least a few days after working a 100k and maybe even a week after a 100 miler. So I think these rates should be 2-3x to account for that. We should have a separate, lower editing day rate when we’re in the comfort of our homes doing a normal 7h day, but the out-in-the-field rate should reflect the time it takes out of your workable days.</p><figure><img src="https://ik.imagekit.io/tony/blog/a-deep-dive-into-day-rates/80fd5249-ff66-4a02-b1bf-07d6025e3285_2731x4096.jpeg?tr=w-1456" alt="A Deep Dive into Day Rates" loading="lazy"></figure><h3>How do we grow?</h3><p>All this and we haven’t even talked about skills, quality of work, proprietary knowledge and access, or the long term toll it takes on our bodies all of which increases with time in the industry. Eventually, you might be starting a family and buy a home. Totally normal things. It feels wild that I might have to justifying this.</p><p>These numbers of $1000-2000 day rates are good for new people coming into this industry but eventually your skills and knowledge grow and your expenses with kids and a mortgage grow. $3000-4000 day rates should be normal, but this is around the range where I’ve seen push back from brands. Perhaps that’s because I’m working events and the real money is in campaigns. It’s also because the Outdoor Industry just isn’t as mature as others. I know Tech has even higher rates.</p><h3>The Perfect Day Rate</h3><p>Without a guide at my side, I went from taking whatever I could get to eventually standardizing on 1k, then 1.5k the next year, and after a couple years of that, I’m mostly using 2k with some exceptions depending on a lot of factors including the client size, the difficulty of the work, my current workload, and of course, does it sound fun? One metric I’ve used to grow, is to just keep raising your day rate until you start getting too many “No’s.” Good for experimenting but a bit nerve wrecking if you can’t hear too many “no’s” before you worry about rent checks.</p><p>The perfect day rate, though, is the confluence of happy client and happy you. It’s as much as the client can pay while being happy with the deliverables they get. It’s the lowest you’re willing to take and still feel valued and supported to do your best work. If anything, I think clients should be overpaying as it’s not really their money and a creative who’s happy and feels supported is way more likely to work harder and overdeliver than one that isn’t.</p><figure><img src="https://ik.imagekit.io/tony/blog/a-deep-dive-into-day-rates/dbd3a53c-376f-4bda-b2cc-7d5f7b473dc4_2731x4096.jpeg?tr=w-1456" alt="A Deep Dive into Day Rates" loading="lazy"></figure><h1>Considerations</h1><p>There is a lot more to charging for your work than what I wrote above so you’ll also have to consider things like:</p><ul><li><p>Can the client pay this? Is it worth this to them? There are companies of all sizes and appreciations of media so don’t take it personally if they can’t. You can decide to lower your rates to them, negotiate, educate, or simply move on knowing they just aren’t the right client for you.</p></li><li><p>I’ve seen invoices where someone’s “Creative Fee” for a day was 30k. Just for that one person. While I aspire to this level, much of the kind of work I do with events in the outdoor industry just isn’t ready for those numbers. Perhaps other industries and campaign work are better places to get those rates.</p></li><li><p>I could be very wrong about all this and if I am, someone please tell me so I can raise my rates lol.</p></li><li><p>Above I estimate 10% for savings which is 350 in those numbers. I put that into a compound investing calculator at 8% and it gets you to just over a million in 40 years. I don’t know about you, but a mil doesn’t feel as big as it used to. Realistically, this should be bigger. Something to raise as you grow. Also, I started this career in my 30s so I don’t even have 30 years left.</p></li><li><p>I’m sure you can point out a lot of errors in my math here. I could too. This is a back-of-the-napkin estimation session so chill.</p></li><li><p>Estimating 7 hours for a “day” as you need a lunch break! Not including commuting time.</p></li><li><p>None of this is to say I don’t love what I do. Ya, I could make money easier another way, probably. But that’s not the point. It’s only advantageous for brands and for other freelancers to be building sustainable careers. The industry won’t do well if everyone has to grow out of it in 5-10 years.</p></li><li><p>An alternative to Day Rates is Value-Based Pricing. While I think it would be great to know how much money companies make from the work I produce and charge accordingly, this information is very hard to get from a brand and sometimes they don’t even know.</p></li></ul><figure><img src="https://ik.imagekit.io/tony/blog/a-deep-dive-into-day-rates/e11009ec-d033-4adf-a816-114017a4ca2b_3399x2233.jpeg?tr=w-1456" alt="A Deep Dive into Day Rates" loading="lazy"></figure><h1>Why share this?</h1><p>I hope it’s obvious but just in case, I’m sharing this so that freelancers and clients everywhere can work better together. Clients might not know and may need a good gauge on what it will cost to hire people so they can factor it into their budgets earlier on. Freelancers new to the space will have no idea what to charge and most will default low because 90% of people feel imposter syndrome or think they aren’t worth that much especially if you are a normal person who still thinks $1000 is like a ton of money. For us, it might be, for many companies, it’s not. The more new freelancers know about how to charge appropriately, the less likely the rest of the industry will be undercut.</p><h1>Will you share with me?</h1><p>Probably should have done this before writing the post but here we are. Share this post or at least this survey with other people in the industry. The more responses we get we better the findings will be!</p><p class="not-prose my-6 text-center"><a class="inline-block px-5 py-3 rounded bg-blue-500 text-white font-sans font-bold no-underline hover:bg-blue-400" href="https://forms.gle/q1NprVc3us6MbeEYA" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Day Rate Survey</a></p><p></p>
